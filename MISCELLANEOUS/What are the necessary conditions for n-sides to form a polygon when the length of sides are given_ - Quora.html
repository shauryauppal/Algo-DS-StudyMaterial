Intuitive Answer:

If you have n sides and you want to form a closed polygon; the boundary case when you would be barely able to "close" the polygon is that, n - 1 sides are next to each other along the same line and the last side is exactly as long as the sum of the previous sides. The polygon you get is basically a straight line with longest side overlapping on other sides.

If the last side be any greater than the sum, it would be impossible to close the polygon. So, the longest side should not be larger than the sum of other sides.
If you want the polygon not to be reduced to a straight line, you make the inequality strict: the longest side should be smaller than the sum of other sides.

The largest side being smaller than the sum of the other sides is necessary and sufficient.  This is true whether the order of the sides is given or not.

The triangle inequality gives the proof.  Iteratively merge the smallest edge with its smaller neighbor until there are three edges.  Each new edge is no greater than the sum of the remaining edges (the sum of the smallest edge and anything other than the largest edge cannot exceed the sum of all edges when there are four or more edges).  At the end you have three side lengths that obey the a <= b + c.

In the case that a = b + c then you need to add a slight kink to the largest edge to make it effectively an epsilon smaller.  You can always do this because the largest edge must be made of multiple input edges; otherwise the condition at the top would be violated.

Thanks for the A2A.
As Anurag's answer points out, any side of the polygon should be smaller than sum of remaining sides.  This should be verified for all sides. There are various ways to explain this:
1. You can divide a given polygon into triangles and use the triangle inequality again and again:
                      Assume a pentagon AB-BC-CD-DE-EA.
                      AC < AB + BC
                      AD < AC + CD => AD < AB + BC + CD (because AC < AB + BC)
                       AE < AD + DE => AE < AB + BC + CD + DE.

2. This is a general trick in vector displacements:
                       Say you are gvien a vector displacements between 2 points A and E,    →AEAE→.
                       Now, you can either choose to displace from A to E directly or choose to go from A to E via multiple other points, your final displacement remains the same, so
                    →AE=→AB+→BEAE→=AB→+BE→ (speaking of it at a linguistic level, you add an alphabet between A and E, once after A once before E)
                     →AE=→AB+→BC+→CD+→DEAE→=AB→+BC→+CD→+DE→
              Now since, |⃗x+⃗y|<=|⃗x|+|⃗y||x→+y→|<=|x→|+|y→|, where the equality holds only in case of a x and y being parallel,
                    AE<=AB+BC+CD+DEAE<=AB+BC+CD+DE,
This is true for any given 5 points. For a polygon, none of 3 points are in a straight line, so
AE<AB+BC+CD+DEAE<AB+BC+CD+DE.  More over this irrespective of the polygon is convex or concave.
